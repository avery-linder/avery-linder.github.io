Do More Shots or More Accurate Shots Produce More Goals in the NHL?

nhl
hockey
Author

Avery Linder

Published

December 5, 2025

In hockey, it’s easy to assume that the team with the most shots or the most goals will dominate the whole season. Fans, journalists, and even some players often measure a team’s success by their offensive output, because more shots should lead to more goals, right?

But in reality, in order to win the Stanley Cup teams must have a balanced offense with efficiency, strong defensive play, and depth to succeed in the league.

Over the past five seasons, the league has had some interesting patterns. Some teams have high shot totals but they don’t always lead in goals or wins. Whereas, a few teams have moderate shot totals and often rank near the top of shooting efficiency, and sometimes championships.

This raises a key question: what matters more in the NHL? Shot volume or shooting accuracy? Or is it a combination of different factors that separates top teams from the rest?

First, let’s look at shooting accuracy. Some teams score more efficiently than others, making each shot count. This efficiency can separate good teams from great ones and often explains why some teams succeed even without taking the most shots.

While shooting accuracy highlights which teams make the most of their opportunities, it doesn’t tell the full story. Does taking more shots actually result in more goals? Not always.

Some teams with moderate shot totals still rank near the top in goals per game, while others with high shot volume struggle to convert.

Teams such as the Tampa Bay Lightning score at high rates without taking the most amount of shots, showing that efficiency matters more than just the volume.

Ultimately, the main purpose of taking shots and scoring goals is to win games.

Interestingly, not all Stanley Cup winners rank at the top in shooting efficiency. For example, the Florida Panthers, are ranked 28th out of 32 teams in shooting efficiency, yet they’ve won the Stanley Cup twice in the past five years This shows that while scoring efficiently helps, balanced team play is a key factor for winning championships.

Let’s take a look at wins versus losses for all teams in the NHL over the past 5 years, showing which teams turn goals into wins and which teams struggle to do so.

Specifically, where do the past five Stanley Cup Winners rank amongst the rest of the league?

Code 
stats <- read_csv("SPMC 350 Project #2 Data - Sheet4.csv")

wins_losses <- stats |>
  group_by(Team) |>
  summarise(
    Wins = sum(W),
    Losses = sum(L)
  )

champs <- wins_losses |>
  filter(Team == "Vegas Golden Knights" | Team == "Florida Panthers" | Team == "Colorado Avalanche" | Team == "Tampa Bay Lightning")

ggplot() +
  geom_dumbbell(
    data = wins_losses,
    aes(y = reorder(Team, Wins), x = Wins, xend = Losses),
    size = 1.75,
    colour = "#C0C0C0",
    colour_x = "#808080",
    colour_xend = "#71797E",
    alpha = .3
  ) +
  geom_dumbbell(
    data = champs,
    aes(y = reorder(Team, Wins), x = Wins, xend = Losses),
    size = 1.75,
    colour = "darkgrey",
    colour_x = "green",
    colour_xend = "red"
  ) +
  labs(
    x="Wins vs Losses", 
    y="", 
    title="High Offense Doesn’t Guarantee a Championship", 
    subtitle="The Tampa Bay Lightning are the only recent Stanley Cup champions to rank high in shots, goals, and shooting \npercentage, highlighting that winning the Cup also depends on defense, special teams, and team depth.", 
    caption="Source: Hockey Reference | By Avery Linder"

Overall, success in the NHL isn’t just about taking more shots or scoring the most goals.

Efficiency does help, but it’s not the only factor that a balanced team needs. A strong defense, special teams, and roster depth all play a crucial role in a teams’ performance.

Teams like the Tampa Bay Lightning show that high efficiency and well-rounded play turn into championships. At the same time, the Florida Panthers’ highlight that winning in hockey is about more than just numbers on the scoreboard.

In the NHL, balanced game play often matters more than just taking the highest amount of shots.
